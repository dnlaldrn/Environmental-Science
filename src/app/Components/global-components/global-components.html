The Green House Effect

The greenhouse effect is the process where Earth’s atmosphere traps heat, similar to how glass traps heat in a greenhouse. Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms Earth’s surface. Some of this heat escapes into space, while the rest is absorbed by greenhouse gases in the troposphere, warming the air. The main greenhouse gases are water vapor, carbon dioxide, chlorofluorocarbons, methane, and nitrous oxide, with water vapor and carbon dioxide responsible for most heat absorption.

Measuring Carbon Dioxide in the Atmosphere

In 1958, geochemist Charles David Keeling set up an instrument on Mauna Loa to measure global carbon dioxide levels far from local influences like forests and cities. His first reading showed 314 parts per million of carbon dioxide. Over time, he observed a seasonal pattern: levels decrease in summer as plants absorb carbon dioxide through photosynthesis, and increase in winter as plant decay releases carbon dioxide back into the air.

Rising Carbon Dioxide Levels

After a few years of measurements, Charles David Keeling discovered that carbon dioxide levels were not only changing seasonally but also increasing overall each year. Winter levels kept getting higher, and summer levels did not drop as much. From 1958 to 2000, carbon dioxide rose from 314 to about 368 parts per million—an increase of 54 ppm (17%). This long-term rise is likely caused by the burning of fossil fuels.

Green House Gases and the Earth's Temperature

Scientists believe that increasing greenhouse gases leads to higher global temperatures because these gases trap heat near Earth’s surface. Evidence from the past 400,000 years shows a strong link between carbon dioxide levels and global temperatures. Today, human activities—such as burning fossil fuels in power plants and cars, and deforestation—release large amounts of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. In addition, other greenhouse gases like CFCs, methane, and nitrous oxide are also being emitted in significant quantities, further contributing to global warming.

How Certain Is Global Warming?

The increase of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere is believed to raise Earth’s average temperature, a phenomenon known as global warming. Data shows that global temperatures increased during the 20th century, and many scientists expect this trend to continue into the 21st century. However, some scientists argue that this warming may be part of natural climate variations, noting that Earth has experienced significant temperature changes throughout its history.

Scientists cannot accurately predict the exact rate of global warming because Earth’s climate system is highly complex and involves many interacting variables. To study it, they use computer models as shown in Figure 3 that simulate the atmosphere and oceans by incorporating data like carbon dioxide levels, winds, and other factors. These models estimate future changes in temperature, rainfall, and sea levels. However, predictions are complicated by feedback processes—such as changes in clouds, water vapor, ice, oceans, and vegetation—which can alter outcomes. Despite these challenges, climate models are becoming more reliable as better data, more variables, and faster computers are used.

Recent Findings

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is a network of approximately 2,500 of the world’s leading climatologists from 70 countries. In 2001, the IPCC issued its Third Assessment Report (TAR). TAR describes what is currently known about the global climate system and provides future estimates about the state of the global climate system. Some of the findings of the IPCC included that the average global surface temperature increased by 0.6°C during the 20th century.

In 1997, 160 countries created the Kyoto Protocol, a treaty requiring developed nations to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to an average of 5% below 1990 levels by 2012, once ratified by 55% of participating countries. Although most developed nations supported it, the United States chose not to ratify the treaty in 2001. To help slow global warming, some countries and organizations have started reforestation projects to absorb carbon dioxide, but progress is challenging because of economic, political, and social differences, especially conflicts between developed and developing nations over future CO₂ emissions.
