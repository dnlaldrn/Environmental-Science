Climate Determinants

The Power of Latitude

Latitude is the distance of a place north or south of the equator, measured from 0° to 90°. It is the most critical factor affecting climate because it determines the intensity of solar energy a location receives.

Low Latitudes (Equator)

Sunlight strikes directly, concentrating energy on a smaller area. This results in consistently high temperatures, 12-hour days, and no distinct seasonal shifts.

High Latitudes (Poles)

Sunlight reaches Earth at an angle, spreading energy over a larger area. This causes colder temperatures and extreme variations in daylight hours between summer and winter.

Global Mechanics

Atmospheric Circulation

How the movement of air and Earth's rotation shape the world's precipitation and wind patterns.

Density & Temperature

Cold air is dense and sinks, while warm air rises and expands. This basic movement is the engine of all weather.

Moisture Capacity

Warm air holds more water vapor. As it rises and cools, vapor condenses into precipitation—explaining heavy rain near the equator.

Solar Heating

Solar energy creates pressure differences. Combined with Earth's rotation, this creates the global wind patterns we see today.

Global Circulation Cells

30°

Air sinks and warms, creating dry conditions. This is why most deserts are found at these latitudes.

60°

Warm air meets cold polar air and rises, leading to cloud formation and heavy precipitation.

The Poles: Cold air sinks, creating "Polar Deserts" that are both freezing and dry.

Atmospheric Physics

Prevailing
Winds

Prevailing winds are winds that consistently blow in one direction throughout the year.

The Coriolis Effect

Due to Earth’s rotation, these winds are deflected:

  • → Toward the Right in the Northern Hemisphere
  • ← Toward the Left in the Southern Hemisphere

Visualizing the deflection of Trade Winds and Westerlies.

The Three Prevailing Winds

0-30

Trade Winds

Blowing between the equator and 30°. Northeast in the North; Southeast in the South.

30-60

Westerlies

Blowing from the southwest in the North and northwest in the South. Key for mid-latitude weather.

60-90

Polar Easterlies

Blowing from the poles toward 60°. Bringing cold, dry air toward the warmer zones.

Note: Winds are deflected right in the Northern Hemisphere and left in the Southern Hemisphere due to the Coriolis effect.

Oceanic Circulation

The Global Heat Engine: Transporting Energy Across the Planet

Water can store and transport massive amounts of heat. Surface currents, driven by wind and Earth’s rotation, redistribute this energy to regulate global temperatures, especially in coastal regions.

El Niño (Warm Phase)

Short-term: 6–18 Months

Warm water shifts toward the eastern Pacific. This triggers a chain reaction in global weather:

  • ↑ Increased Rain: Southern US & South America.
  • ↓ Drought: Indonesia & Australia.

La Niña (Cool Phase)

Short-term: 6–18 Months

The eastern Pacific becomes cooler than normal, creating opposite weather patterns:

  • ↓ Drier Conditions: Parts of the Americas.
  • ↑ Wetter Conditions: SE Asia & Australia.

Topography & External Influences

The Elevation Effect

Elevation dramatically changes climate regardless of latitude. Mount Kilimanjaro sits just 3° south of the equator, yet its peak is covered in snow year-round.

-6°C / 1,000m

Temperatures fall by about 6°C (11°F) for every 1,000-meter increase in height above sea level.

Solar Maximum

The sun follows an 11-year cycle. During a maximum, increased UV radiation produces more ozone, warming the stratosphere and the Earth's surface.

Volcanic Eruptions

Sulfur dioxide gas can reach the stratosphere, forming a haze layer that reflects sunlight, causing global temperatures to decrease for up to three years.
