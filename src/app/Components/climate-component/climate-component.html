Climate and Climate Change

Weather is the state of the atmosphere at a particular place at a particular moment. Climate, on the other hand, refers to the long-term prevailing weather conditions based on recorded data over time.

What Factors Determine Climate?

Climate is influenced by several factors including latitude, atmospheric and oceanic circulation patterns, local geography, solar activity, and volcanic activity.

Latitude is the distance from the equator measured in degrees north or south.

Low Latitudes

Areas near the equator receive more direct sunlight, resulting in higher amounts of solar energy and warmer climates.

High Latitudes

Regions closer to the poles receive less solar energy because the sun appears lower in the sky, leading to cooler climates.

Atmospheric Circulation

Three key properties of air that drive climate circulation:

Cold air sinks: Because it is denser, cold air moves downward, compressing and warming as it drops.
Warm air rises: As it moves upward, it expands and cools.
Moisture capacity: Warm air can hold significantly more water vapor than cold air. Therefore, when warm air cools, it loses its ability to hold that moisture (which ultimately leads to condensation and precipitation).

Wind creation: Solar energy heats the ground and the air above it. As this warm air rises, cooler air rushes in to replace it, creating wind.

Oceanic Circulation Patterns

Ocean currents have a great effect on climate because water holds large amounts of heat.

The ENSO Cycle: A periodic, short-term (6- to 18-month) shift in the location of warm and cold water masses across the Pacific Ocean.

El Niño (Warm Phase): Stronger winds push warm water eastward. This alters global weather, causing increased rainfall in the southern U.S. and equatorial South America, while bringing drought to Indonesia and Australia.

La Niña (Cold Phase): The opposite extreme, where water in the eastern Pacific Ocean becomes unusually cool.

Phase Comparison: El Niño represents the warm phase and La Niña represents the cold phase of the ENSO cycle, as illustrated in Figure 5.

Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO)

The PDO Cycle: A long-term shift—lasting 20 to 30 years—in the location of warm and cold water masses in the Pacific Ocean.

Climate Impacts: It significantly influences the climate of the northern Pacific Ocean and North America by affecting ocean surface temperatures, air temperatures, and precipitation patterns.

Topography

Elevation significantly impacts climate, causing temperatures to drop by about 6°C for every 1,000 meters of height. This explains why high-altitude peaks like Mount Kilimanjaro maintain year-round snow despite sitting near the equator.

Other major climate drivers include solar activity and volcanic eruptions. During a solar maximum, the sun emits increased UV radiation that produces more ozone and ultimately warms the atmosphere. On the other hand, large volcanic eruptions can actually lower global temperatures. They release sulfur dioxide into the upper atmosphere, which reacts to form a bright, persistent haze that reflects incoming sunlight away from the Earth for up to three years.

Seasonal changes in temperature and precipitation are driven by Earth's 23.5° axial tilt as it orbits the sun. This tilt changes the angle and duration of sunlight reaching the surface. As shown in Figure 4, when a hemisphere tilts toward the sun, it experiences summer, receiving more direct sunlight and longer hours of daylight to heat the Earth.

Simultaneously, the opposite hemisphere tilts away, receiving less direct sunlight and experiencing winter. This cycle completely reverses as the Earth continues its orbit around the sun.
